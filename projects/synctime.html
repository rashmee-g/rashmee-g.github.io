SyncTime

A Google Calendar “social extension” concept that helps groups find mutual availability quickly without the back-and-forth texting.

My Role

UI/UX Designer (research → wireframes → prototype), working with a 4-person design team through feedback cycles and a final presentation.

Goal

Make scheduling feel “built-in” to the calendar: faster overlaps, fewer messages, and clearer next steps.

Problem

Even when everyone uses Google Calendar, planning something with friends or a group often turns into texting: “Are you free?” “What about Tuesday?” “No, I’m busy.” SyncTime focuses on one pain point which is quickly finding overlapping availability, and turning it into a simple, guided flow.

Research & Key Insights

We used survey research to understand how people currently coordinate and what gets in the way.

  • 47 total responses (primary demographic: 18–22)
  • 97.9% rely on messaging apps to coordinate availability
  • 66% don’t use Guest Permissions (often too hidden/complex)
  • 68.1% rated Google Calendar 4/5 → the issue is a missing “group scheduling” layer

User Persona

We grounded the concept around a user who already uses calendar tools, but still ends up coordinating in messages.

Design Process

We explored how to keep the experience lightweight: compare schedules, surface overlaps, and suggest next steps without overwhelming users with settings. The final direction focuses on clarity, speed, and “what do I do now?”

Design Decisions

  • Reduce steps: pick group → see overlap → pick time
  • Show “best options” first instead of making users hunt
  • Keep it friendly + social (not corporate scheduling)

What I’d Measure Next

  • Time to schedule (start → confirmed time)
  • Confusion points (where users hesitate)
  • Preference: suggested times vs manual selection

Reflection & Next Steps

If I continued this project, I’d validate the flow with a few quick usability tests and then tighten the experience around edge cases.

What I Learned

  • “Scheduling” is emotional; people want fast clarity, not more settings.
  • Small UX changes can remove a ton of coordination friction.
  • Presenting design decisions clearly matters as much as making the screens.

Next Features

  • Time zone handling and “best times” ranking
  • Availability preferences (e.g., “no mornings”)
  • Lightweight reminders + shareable summary
